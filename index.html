About

Inclusionomics™ is a collective think tank born out of the desire to see positive change in the advancement of women, professionals of color and other underrepresented and marginalized professionals. We can no longer attempt to tackle the issues of diversity, equality and discrimination one at a time. These issues need to be addressed simultaneously and for that, we need engagement on many levels – from CEO’s to activists to educators to researchers – Inclusionomics is igniting the conversations necessary to effect change.

Increasing gender & pay equity

Women make up 50% of the workforce yet they are paid less than men. As the main, or sole source of income in many households, women are retiring after working their entire lives and are living below the poverty level. We cannot hope to change economic outcomes without tackling this issue.

Tackling discrimination & sexual harassment

We believe in working to create workplaces that prevent sexual harassment and discrimination rather than managing workplaces that need to continually address the issues after it happens. Now is the time for change. Work with us to maintain a respectful workplace for all employees.

Creating diverse workforces and inclusive workplaces

Gender, race, ethnicity, age, socioeconomic background and ability are all aspects of our lives that affect how we’re treated. You can be a catalyst for change within your organization, community or within yourself. Diversity is not just a buzzword, inclusion is intentional and both are a way of life.

Why Should I Help?

Inclusionomics cannot show the sheer volume of incidences that occur on a daily basis without your testimonials. We suspect you leave your company because you don’t feel heard. But we hear you. And we want others to hear you too. In a 2017 study, nearly eight in 10 employees who left tech jobs reported experiencing some form of unfair behavior or treatment, while 85% observed it. And 37% said they left their jobs because of it.

We would appreciate you taking a moment to answer a few questions. This is a first step towards helping us to help companies address discrimination at work and build diverse workplaces.

We respect your privacy and understand that anonymity is vital, which is why our database does not link your name and email address to the demographic data or the testimonials you provide.

Share My Story

Stories

As a deaf blind person, I sat on a plane for 13+ hours not knowing where anything was - that includes safety information, toilets, no access to entertainment system, no access to assistance at all. The 13+ hour flight was a nightmare, I am completely blind in the dark and as a result of aircraft noise, little hearing and no access to lipreading, my experience was not just horrible but uncomfortable and unpleasant. I cannot make head nor tail of this organisation, I simply asked for ‘Reasonable Adjustments” not because I’m difficult but because I want to feel comfortable just like everybody else.
I had worked for my company for a few years and a position opened that I thought would be a great fit for me. I spoke to my direct supervisor about it and he agreed I should go for it. I scheduled a meeting with the head of our department to discuss it and I hadn’t been in her office for more than a couple of minutes before she told me that the position involved a lot of travel and I had young children at home so this wouldn’t be a fit for me. I started looking for a new job the next day.
To be fair, I don't know why that saleswoman didn't want to help me. I suspect it's because I'm black, but it could also be because I'm fat. Maybe my whole life, every time I thought someone was being racist, they were actually mistreating me because I'm fat. That sucks too. That's not OK.
I was in my early 20s and I was looking for a secretarial job. Executive assistant roles pay more and when I asked a recruiter about those types of opportunities, she looked me up and down and said, you don't have the right look for that job. Not that I don't have the right skills, but that I don't have the right look. So I look like a secretary but not an executive assistant? What the hell does that even mean?
